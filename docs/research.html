Dissertation Research

Post-Fire Rain-on-Snow Hydrology in the Eastern Cascades

Wildfire fundamentally alters the forest canopy that regulates snowpack energy balance. In the Eastern Cascades, where rain-on-snow events drive some of the most dangerous flood and debris flow hazards, understanding how canopy loss changes snowmelt timing and magnitude is an urgent and open question.

My dissertation uses remote sensing, field instrumentation, and long-term streamflow records to quantify these effects across a gradient of burn severity at two fire sites in Washington State.

Remote Sensing Tools

  • MODIS fSCA — fractional snow cover area time series
  • Sentinel-1 SAR — wet snow detection
  • Landsat dNBR — burn severity mapping
  • RAVG CC-5 — canopy cover change post-fire
  • Google Earth Engine — cloud-based processing platform

Monitoring Network

Field instrumentation deployed at Labor Mountain and Lower Sugarloaf includes timelapse cameras, snow stakes, tipping bucket precipitation gauges, pressure transducers, and temperature/humidity loggers. Reference SNOTEL stations: Pope Ridge, Harts Pass, Rainy Pass, and Blewett Pass.

Federal Research Context

This work is embedded in my role as BAER (Burned Area Emergency Response) Coordinator at the Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest, where post-fire watershed assessment directly informs land management decisions and public safety. My dissertation bridges the gap between operational emergency response and long-term hydrologic research.

Tribal partnerships with the Yakama Nation and Colville Confederated Tribes inform field site access and research priorities.
